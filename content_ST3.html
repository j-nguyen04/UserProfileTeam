Life Below Water

Overview of Life Below Water

Life Below Water was established in 2015 by the United Nations and explains about conserving and sustainably use of oceans, sea and marine resources in order to achieve sustainable development.

Ocean Pollution

Ocean Pollution is one of the main threats to marine life and ecosystems.
Plastics end up in the ocean through various factors
such as littering, and poor waste management which contribute for the pollution.
Plastic items such as shopping bags, bottle caps and many more - they pose a threat
because it is long-lasting and so will take years to decompose

Solutions against marine pollution include prevention and cleanup to reduce pollution
from industrial and agricultural activities which can promote waste reduction,
raising public awareness of the impacts of ocean pollution.

Climate Change

Climate Change is affecting marine ecosystems such as ocean acidification.
Ocean acidification is caused by the ocean absorbing excess carbon dioxide from the atmostphere.
This can create conditions that eat away marine life such as oysters, clams, and lobsters.

Scientists are monitoring and studying these causes through isotopic and other techniques to understand
how ocean acidification is posing a threat towards
marine life and other ecosystems and find alternatives to protect the ocean.

Overfishing

Overfishing is a global problem that threatens ocean wildlife and biodiversity.
Causing loss of marine life pose threats towards people who rely on marine life to gain protein.
Overfishing occurs when too many fish are
caught to a point where adults cannot breed and sustain a healthy population.

The level of overfishing has increased and the number of overfished stocks is three times higher than in 1970.
Studies from United Nations have monitored around 500 stocks of fish around the globe and in 2022,
and is estimated more than a third of global fish stocks are overfished

Coastal Eutrophication

Eutrophication is caused by excessive plant and algae growth due to increased availability
of one or more limiting growth factors to achieve photosynthesis.
This creates problems, including dead zones reducing fish and shellfish production.
Harmful algae blooms that form taste and odor problems - this threatens the safety of drinking water supplies.

Ways to mitigate against eutrophication could reduce the input of nitrogren and
phosphorus into coastal waters from industrial and agricultural sources.
they could also encourage sustainable agricultural practices to reduce nutrient run off and soil erosion
